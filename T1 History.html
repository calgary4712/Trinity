Part I.1 - Brief History

Here we will attempt a brief history of the development of the doctrine of the Trinity. The goal is to cover the main points of the development from the time of Christ to the Council of Constantinople 381 A.D. when the Nicene Creed was completed.

Israel at the time of Christ.

If we are to understand the words and actions of Jesus we need to have an understanding of the context in which they were done. To that end, lets have a brief look at the main points of the Jewish religion of that time.


One God. The Jewish people believed in one God: “Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one.” (Deut 6:4). The word Lord is a stand in for YHWH the sacred name revealed to Moses:


“God said to Moses, "I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: “I AM has sent me to you.” (Exodus 3:14).

This name is so sacred that it is not to be pronounced, instead Lord (Adonai) is to be used. Only the high priest could pronounce YHWH once a year in the Holy of Holies during the solemn feast of Atonement.


Covenant. With Moses as the mediator, the Lord God made a covenant with the Israelites. The Lord would be their God and they would be his people. He would bless them, make them prosper and multiply, and protect them. They in their turn were to keep the Ten Commandments. This they did not do. Their history is one of chasing after other gods, breaking the commandments, being chastised, and returning back to God. Through the prophets, God promised them a new covenant:


Behold, the days are coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers on the day when I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt, my covenant that they broke, though I was their husband, declares the Lord. For this is the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel after those days, declares the Lord: I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts. And I will be their God, and they shall be my people. (Jeremiah 31:31-33).

Torah. Torah is the teaching, the way, the law, based on the ten commandments. It shows how to be a righteous one before God and guides every aspect of daily life.


Forgiveness. With the high standards set by the ten commandments comes a greater awareness of sin. The book of Leviticus prescribes the animal sacrifices that are to be made for forgiveness of sin. Over time the meaning of sacrifice would deepen:


For You do not delight in sacrifice, otherwise I would give it;
You are not pleased with burnt offering.
The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit;
A broken and a contrite heart, O God, You will not despise (Psalm 51:16-17).

Holy Days. The two main holy days were the Passover and Yom Kipper. The Passover meal was the celebration of freedom from slavery in Egypt. The sacrificial animal for the meal was usually a year old male lamb. The most solemn holy day was Yom Kippur – the Day of Atonement. Two goats were used, one was sacrificed for the sins of the people and over the other the sins of the people were confessed and the animal was released in the wilderness. As mentioned above, on this day only the High Priest was allowed to say the sacred name of God as revealed to Moses.


Prophets. The prophets were God's word to the people calling them to be faithful to the Covenant. When the people were unfaithful they were warned of punishments to come and when under punishment they were given assurance of God's steadfast love and mercy. God would never abandon them despite their failings. There were still future blessings in store, the greatest of these and most hoped for was the coming of the Messiah.


Of the greatness of his government and peace there will be no end. He will reign on David's throne and over his kingdom, establishing and upholding it with justice and righteousness from that time on and forever. (Isaiah 9:7)

Temple. The temple in Jerusalem, built by Herod using beautiful large white marble slabs, was the central place of worship. The high point of the liturgical year was the Passover celebration with pilgrims coming to Jerusalem from all over the nation and beyond to take part in the Passover meal which required the slaughter of a lamb.


Sabbath. The Sabbath, from Friday evening to Saturday evening, was a day of rest and worship. On the seventh day God stopped creating and rested and so on the Sabbath it was forbidden to do any sort of work since work creates something new.


Family. The importance of the family in Jewish life is nicely expressed by Rabbi David, a contemporary writer


In other words, sanctification is the actual reason for the very existence of the Jewish People. It is thus not without significance that the Hebrew word for Jewish marriage (Kiddushin) means “holiness or sanctification”. Not only is the relationship of marital commitment itself seen as holy - indeed it is seen as the ideal state of adult life - but the family as the central institution and focus of Jewish life, is the key to the realization of the people’s raison d’etre, to be “a holy nation.” (The Family in Judaism)

Love of Neighbor. The Book of Leviticus 19:18 gives the golden rule about loving your neighbor as yourself.


Summary. Such, briefly, are the main points of Jewish religious life in Palestine at the time of Christ. One cannot speak of Jesus' time without also mentioning the hated Roman occupation since it was the Roman occupation that flamed the hopes for the coming of the Messiah, one who would free them from Rome. It was this false understanding of the Messiah that would be used against Jesus.

Jesus' Public Ministry

Enter Jesus of Nazareth in the setting described above. We will now look at what Jesus said and did regarding the main points of Jewish life. For an excellent write up on this topic check out Pope Benedict's book Jesus of Nazareth , Holy Week.


Messianic Titles. First we need to look at how Jesus referred to himself. There was a high expectation that the Messiah would come soon to restore Israel to the rule of David's line. This was a promise God made through Nathan the prophet to King David.


... I will raise up your offspring to succeed you, one of your own sons... and I will establish his throne forever. I will be his father, and he will be my son. I will never take my love away from him (1Chron 17: 11-14)

So when the title 'Son of God' is applied by people to Jesus in the gospels it is meant as a Davidic title and not as a divine person as it would later come to mean in Christianity. Jesus avoided such titles and would instead call himself 'Son of Man'. Look at how Jesus corrects Nathaneal's messianic titles:


Then Nathanael declared, “Rabbi, you are the Son of God; you are the king of Israel.” Jesus said, “You believe because I told you I saw you under the fig tree. You will see greater things than that.” He then added, “Very truly I tell you, you will see heaven open, and the angels of God ascending and descending on the Son of Man. (John 1:49-53)”.

Nathanael puts the titles 'Son of God' and 'King of Israel' together in the same breath. Such an interpretation of Jesus' ministry would mean a military overthrow of the occupying Roman army. Jesus avoids these references and instead speaks of heaven opening and angels ascending and descending on the Son of Man.


And when Jesus is questioned by the High Priest


Again the high priest asked him, “Are you the Messiah, the Son of the Blessed One?” “I am,” said Jesus. “And you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven.” (Mark 14:61-62)

The high priest, like Nathanael, puts Messiah and Son of the Blessed One (Son of God) together. And again Jesus responds with a reference to heaven and the 'Son of Man'.


The expression 'Son of Man' is from Daniel who after having a vision of four beasts, representing earthly kingdoms that ruled with cruelty, finally sees:


In my vision at night I looked, and there before me was one like a son of man, coming with the clouds of heaven. He approached the Ancient of Days and was led into his presence. He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all nations and peoples of every language worshiped him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that will not pass away, and his kingdom is one that will never be destroyed (Daniel 7:13-14).

Daniel is told that the vision of 'a son of man' represents the holy people of the Most High who will receive the kingdom and will possess it forever' (v18)”. Jesus modifies it to 'the Son of Man' when referring to himself. Why did he use this title? I suppose because in Daniel's vision there is no reference to political or military authority - King David had a long list of military conquests. In the vision of Daniel, the Son of Man's authority comes not from the number of followers or the size of his army but from the Ancient of Days. Also, the Son of Man comes on the 'clouds of heaven'; he comes from above. In Jerusalem, Jesus says to the Jews:


For just as the Father has life in himself, so he has granted the Son also to have life in himself; and he has given him authority to execute judgment, because he is the Son of Man.(John 5:26-27)
Then what if you were to see the Son of Man ascending to where he was before?(John 6:62)
“You are from below; I am from above. You are of this world; I am not of this world” (John 8:23).

In the next part, where we consider what Jesus said and did, the title 'Son of Man' will frequently occur.


One God. In regards to the Lord God, Jesus would dare to call him 'abba' - a term of endearment and respect that a son would use towards his father. This would be respectful towards a human father but to call the God of Israel with such familiarity would, I think, be bordering on disrespect in that setting. And to make matters worse he says “The Father and I are one (John 10:30)” making himself equal to the Lord God.


The Sacred Name. In the temple for all to hear, Jesus says “Very truly I tell you, before Abraham was, I AM! (John 8:58)”. He says the unutterable divine name, the name that only the high priest was allowed to say, the name of the Lord God, “I AM” , and applies it to himself. One can hear gasps across the centuries.


Torah. In regards to Torah Jesus would say:

You have heard that it was said, ‘Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' “But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven. (Mat 5:43-45)

Several times, in Matthew's Sermon on the Mount, Jesus states the old sacred teaching and replaces it with a new one, on no authority other then “I tell you”.


And just as the Torah was meant to last, so were his words.


Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. For truly, I say to you, until heaven and earth pass away, not an iota, not a dot, will pass from the Law until all is accomplished. (Matt 5:17-18)
Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away. (Matt 24:35)

Torah was the way, a light that guides one on the path to being righteous before God. Jesus states that he is Torah, that he is the way to the Father and just as the Father is life itself so he claims to be life.


I am the way, and the truth, and the life; no one comes to the Father but through Me. (John 14:6)

Forgiveness. Scripture is full of statements regarding the Lord God's mercy. A couple of examples:


The LORD is slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love, forgiving iniquity and transgression (Numbers 14:18)
The Lord our God is merciful and forgiving, even though we have rebelled against him (Daniel 9:9).

Forgiveness of sin was from God and God alone yet Jesus dares to say the 'Son of Man' has authority to forgive sins.


When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralyzed man, “Son, your sins are forgiven.” Now some teachers of the law were sitting there, thinking to themselves, “Why does this fellow talk like that? He’s blaspheming! Who can forgive sins but God alone?”. Immediately Jesus knew in his spirit that this was what they were thinking in their hearts, and he said to them, “Why are you thinking these things? Which is easier: to say to this paralyzed man, ‘Your sins are forgiven,’ or to say, ‘Get up, take your mat and walk’? But I want you to know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins.” So he said to the man, “I tell you, get up, take your mat and go home.”. He got up, took his mat and walked out in full view of them all. (Mark 2:5-12)

Prophets. He fulfills what was said by the prophet Isaiah regarding the Messiah


When he came to Nazareth, where he had been brought up, he went to the synagogue on the sabbath day, as was his custom. He stood up to read, and the scroll of the prophet Isaiah was given to him. He unrolled the scroll and found the place where it was written: “The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to bring good news to the poor. He has sent me to proclaim release to the captives and recovery of sight to the blind, to let the oppressed go free, to proclaim the year of the Lord’s favor.” And he rolled up the scroll, gave it back to the attendant, and sat down. The eyes of all in the synagogue were fixed on him. Then he began to say to them, “Today this scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing.(Luke 4:16-21)

Jesus said of John the Baptist that he was a prophet and more than a prophet; he was the messenger sent to prepare the way.


There is another who testifies on my behalf, and I know that his testimony to me is true. You sent messengers to John, and he testified to the truth. Not that I accept such human testimony, but I say these things so that you may be saved. He was a burning and shining lamp, and you were willing to rejoice for a while in his light.(John 5: 32-36)
In Jerusalem he says to the Jews:
You search the scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that testify on my behalf.(John 5:39)

After the resurrection, on the road to Emmaus, he shows to his disciples that all of scripture points to him.


"This is what I told you while I was still with you: Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms." (Luke 24:44)

Temple. In regards to the temple he says: “Destroy this temple, and I will raise it again in three days (John 2:19).”. His contemporaries took it to mean the temple built by Herod but his disciples later realized after the resurrection that he was talking of his body. He was the true temple.


Sabbath. When the Pharisees were condemning his disciples activities on a Sabbath Jesus says, “For the Son of Man is Lord of the Sabbath.” (Matt 12:8) and so he makes himself the authority on proper use of the sabbath.


Family. The bonds between persons in the family were the most important of all human bonds yet Jesus claims an even greater importance.

Whoever loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me; and whoever loves son or daughter more than me is not worthy of me. (Mat 10:37)

Jesus' demand for loyalty is equal to what the Lord God asks in the First Commandment.


New Covenant. At the Last Supper, Jesus says:


And when He had taken a cup and given thanks, He gave it to them, saying, "Drink from it, all of you; for this is My blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for forgiveness of sins. (Mat 28:27-28)

Jesus understands that the shedding of his blood is the blood of the new covenant promised by Jeremiah and that his blood is the blood of atonement.


Love of Neighbor. Jesus says to his disciples:


“I give you a new commandment, that you love one another. Just as I have loved you, you also should love one another. (John 13:35)”.

Jesus makes himself the standard for love of neighbor.


Summary. Through this brief survey we see that Jesus appropriates all the main points of the religious life of Israel:


he and the Lord God are One;

Jesus utters the holy name I AM;

his blood is the new covenant;

the shedding of his blood atones for the sins of the people;

he is Torah;

the prophets speak of him;

he is the temple of God;

he is Lord of the Sabbath;

he is the fulfillment of scripture;

he has the authority to forgive sin;

he is the golden rule.


Some of these words and acts were done only with his followers but most of them were done publicly. Any one of them would be considered blasphemy, collectively they made the penalty for blasphemy a certainty – death.

Early Church

While in hindsight it may seem clear that Jesus was saying he was the Son of the Father and equal to the Father, it was not so clear to the early church - he was a man, after all. In the Jewish faith, unlike the pagan religions, God does not take on human form. The Jewish expectation of the Messiah (anointed one) was a man sent by God to deliver the people, not God in the flesh. The early church certainly regarded Jesus as the Messiah, which in Greek is Christ, and so his name and title became standard – Jesus Christ. But there are texts that show that the early church was taking Jesus' relationship to the Father beyond the limitations of just a human Messiah.


who, though he was in the form of God, did not regard equality with God as something to be exploited (Phil 2:6)

Scholars tell us that this text has the form of a hymn and was most likely used in liturgical worship. Paul is writing of a tradition already established in the early Church.


In the Old Testament the title Lord (Kyrios in Greek) was used when referring to YHWH. In the New Testament it is also applied to Jesus so Jesus' full title became “Lord Jesus Christ”.


Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! (1Peter 1:3)
To the church of the Thessalonians in God the Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. (1 Thessalonians 1:1)
My brothers and sisters, believers in our glorious Lord Jesus Christ (James 2:1)

And then there is the beginning of John's gospel:


In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. (John 1:1).

John's gospel was one of the latest texts to be written (circa 90) and gives a clear indication of the direction the Church's understanding of Jesus was taking.

Nicene Creed – Council of Nicea 325

We now jump to the fourth century. The church was persecuted in the early centuries throughout the Roman Empire making it impossible to hold church councils to deal with church issues. In 313 AD the Edict of Milan was passed which did away with persecution of Christians. At that time, Constantine was emperor of the western Roman empire and in 324 AD, through a series of military victories, he became sole emperor of both the western and eastern Roman empire. The Church had battles of its own as well: there was an intense debate going on regarding the nature of the relationship of the Son to the Father – the issues of the early church were still being explored. In 325 AD the Emperor Constantine called an ecumenical council. For the first time, bishops throughout the empire could gather together.


Regarding the issue of the relationship of the Father to the Son, there were two main positions.


Arius. We will start with Arius' letter to Alexander, bishop of Alexandria. Alexandria was second to Rome in authority.


We acknowledge One God, alone Ingenerate, alone Everlasting, alone Unbegun, alone True, alone having Immortality, alone Wise, alone Good, alone Sovereign; Judge, Governor, and Providence of all, unalterable and unchangeable, just and good, God of Law and Prophets and New Testament.

No problems with this part regarding the nature of God.


who begat an Only-begotten Son before eternal times, through whom He has made both the ages and the universe; and begat Him, not in semblance, but in truth; and that He made Him subsist at His own will, unalterable and unchangeable;

The Son is begotten in truth, which would suggest identical in nature, but then he goes on to say:


God, created before times and before ages, and gaining life and being from the Father, who gave subsistence to His glories together with Him. For the Father did not, in giving to Him the inheritance of all things, deprive Himself of what He has ingenerately in Himself; for He is the Fountain of all things...For He is not eternal or co-eternal or co-unoriginate with the Father, nor has He His being together with the Father, as some speak of relations, introducing two ingenerate beginnings, but God is before all things as being Monad and Beginning of all.

Now the difficulties begin. The Son has “the inheritance of all things”. I suppose we could take this to mean the Son has divine qualities but the Son is not divine like the Father since it is the nature of divinity to be ingenerate and there can only be one who is ingenerate: the Father. Also whatever the Son has from the Father is separate from the Father “nor has He His being together with the Father”.


But if the terms ‘from Him,’ and ‘from the womb,’ and ‘I came forth from the Father, and I am come’ (Rom. xi. 36; Ps. cx. 3; John xvi. 28), be understood by some to mean as if a part of Him, one in essence or as an issue, then the Father is according to them compounded and divisible and alterable and material, and, as far as their belief goes, has the circumstances of a body, Who is the Incorporeal God.

If the Son is one in essence with the Father then all sorts of contradictions arise according to Arius. God is One but if the Son is 'one in essence' with the Father then God is compounded. Also, since the Son is human and so has a material body then God is material, but as Arius rightly insists, God is Incorporeal.


Arius is out to protect the Oneness of God which was the faith of Israel and so obviously there can only be One who is ingenerate. It is only normal to think that One also means one person - it is what human experience teaches us. For each human person there is only one human nature and also for each instance of human nature there is only one person. So if we take seriously that the Son is a different person than the Father, as Arius did, (some didn't) then it follows that the Father has a nature and the Son has a nature and since there can only be one ingenerate divine nature this belongs to the Father alone leaving the Son with a divinity that is not eternal - sort of a divine creature.


Also the issues of Christ's two natures, divine and human, hadn't been worked out yet – that would happen in the following centuries. So, for Arius the Son is a mixture of the divine and the material in one nature and this has no part in an Ingenerate, Incorporeal Divinity.


Athanasius. Athanasius became Bishop of Alexandria (328) and fought vigorously against the Arian position. In 'Oration 3 against the Arians Athanasius defends the divinity of Christ while maintaining God is one.


For even though the Son as begotten is not the Father, nevertheless as God he is the same as the Father, and so he and the Father are one in having the same nature and one and the same divinity, as we have said. For the radiance of the sun is also light, is not later than the sun in time, nor a different light, nor a limited participation of it, but is fully the sun's own offspring. And such an offspring is necessarily one light; it cannot be said that those lights are two lights. The sun and its radiance are indeed two different things, yet there is only one light that comes from the sun and with its radiance illumines the entire world. Thus in a similar way the divinity of the Son is also that of the Father, and for this reason it is also singular, and therefore God is one and there is no other besides him. Hence, because they are one and their divinity is one, it follows that the same things are said of the Son as are said of the Father, with the exception of the name 'Father'... For as the Son himself says, 'All that the Father has are mine'

Athanasius ends up giving a rule that can be used in regarding the relationship between the Father and the Son: “the same things are said of the Son as are said of the Father, with the exception of the name Father”. So just as the Father is Eternal, Almighty, Immutable so is the Son, but anything that has to do with Fatherhood (i.e. beggeting) cannot be applied to the Son. In this way the Father and Son are distinct persons with the same, identical nature.


Creed. The Creed put forth by the council says this about the Son in relation to the Father.


And in one Lord Jesus Christ, the Son of God, begotten from the Father, only-begotten, that is, from the substance of the Father, God from God, light from light, true God from true God, begotten not made, of one substance with the Father…But as for those who say, There was when He was not, and, Before being born He was not, and that He came into existence out of nothing...these the Catholic and Apostolic Church anathematizes.

One would think that the issue would be settled after the Council but instead it became even more intense. Christianity was by now the official religion of the empire and so the Roman emperors felt they had a say in church matters. Several of the emperors following the years after the council were of Arian persuasion and as a result bishops faithful to the Nicene Creed were exiled (Anthanasius was exiled five times from Alexandria). At one point, even the Pope in Rome was exiled and an Arian anti-Pope was set up. In 379 A.D. Theodosius I became emperor who was of the Nicene Creed persuasion. It was under his rule that the next ecumenical council was called.


Nicene Creed - Council of Constantinople 381 AD

The Council of Constantinople reinforces what was stated in the Nicene Creed and goes on to further elaborate about the Holy Spirit. When it came to the Holy Spirit all the Creed of 325 stated was 'and the Holy Spirit'. More needed to be said, but what? We have had a taste of how difficult it was to accept that the Son was God even with statements in the Gospel of John (Word was God) and Paul's letter to the Philippians (equality with God). How much more difficult to accept the divinity of the Holy Spirit who is never called God in the New Testament writing. Some considered the Holy Spirit to be a creature taking John 1:3 (through him all things were made) to include the Holy Spirit as one of those made. Others would not accept that the Holy Spirit was a creature but neither did they say the Holy Spirit was divine.


One of those who considered the Holy Spirit to be God was Basil, Bishop of Caesarea (330-379). As an indication of how heated the debate was at this time, Basil never openly states that the Holy Spirit is God but infers it from his arguments. We include some of them below taken from his work 'De Spiritu Sancto' (Of the Holy Spirit).


24. But we must proceed to attack our opponents... It is not permissible, they assert, for the Holy Spirit to be ranked with the Father and Son, on account of the difference of His nature and the inferiority of His dignity...For if our Lord, when enjoining the baptism of salvation, charged His disciples to baptize all nations in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit not disdaining fellowship with Him, and these men allege that we must not rank Him with the Father and the Son, is it not clear that they openly withstand the commandment of God?
37. Peter's words to Sapphira, “How is it that you have agreed together to tempt the Spirit of the Lord? You have not lied unto men, but unto God”, show that sins against the Holy Spirit and against God are the same; and thus you might learn that in every operation the Spirit is closely conjoined with, and inseparable from, the Father and the Son.
48. Be it so, it is rejoined, but glory is by no means so absolutely due to the Spirit as to require His exaltation by us in doxologies...It is, at all events, possible for us to arrive to a certain extent at intelligent apprehension of the sublimity of His nature and of His unapproachable power, by looking at the meaning of His title, and at the magnitude of His operations, and by His good gifts bestowed on us or rather on all creation. He is called Spirit, as God is a Spirit... He is called holy as the Father is holy , and the Son is holy, for to the creature holiness was brought in from without, but to the Spirit holiness is the fulfillment of nature, and it is for this reason that He is described not as being sanctified, but as sanctifying. He is called good, as the Father is good, and He who was begotten of the Good is good, and to the Spirit His goodness is essence. He is called upright, as the Lord is upright, in that He is Himself truth, and is Himself Righteousness, having no divergence nor leaning to one side or to the other, on account of the immutability of His substance...Thus names are borne by the Spirit in common with the Father and the Son, and He gets these titles from His natural and close relationship. From what other source could they be derived?

Basil is using the rule of Athanasius (see above) and applying it to the Holy Spirit: what is said of the Father and Son can be said of the Holy Spirit except what pertains to Fatherhood and Sonship. With such arguments as these Basil was able to convince others of the divinity of the Holy Spirit and so when the Council of Constantinople was called the statement regarding the Holy Spirit was expanded.


And in the Holy Ghost, the Lord and Giver of life, who proceeds from the Father, who with the Father and the Son together is worshiped and glorified,

Perhaps as a reflection of the times, the Holy Spirit is not explicitly called God but since the Holy Spirit is worshiped and glorified with the Father and Son it can be safely inferred that the Holy Spirit is God along with the Father and Son. And so after the Council of Constantinople, it is official Church teaching that the Father is God, the Son is God and the Holy Spirit is God, all equally God, but there is only one God.
